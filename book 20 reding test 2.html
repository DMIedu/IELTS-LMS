Passage 1 – Manatees
Manatees
Evolutionary biology, physiological traits, and conservation of three manatee species.

Manatees, also known as sea cows, are large, fully aquatic, mostly herbivorous marine mammals. They belong to the order Sirenia, which also includes the dugong and the extinct Steller’s sea cow. There are three living species of manatee: the West Indian manatee (which ranges along the east coast of the Americas), the Amazonian manatee (which lives in the Amazon River basin), and the West African manatee. These gentle creatures are known for their slow-moving nature and their unique physical adaptations to life in the water.

The physical structure of manatees is well suited to their aquatic environment. They have a streamlined body, a large, paddle-shaped tail, and two front flippers, which they use for steering and holding food. Unlike dugongs, which have a notched tail, manatees have a rounded tail that helps them move slowly and stably through shallow waters. Their skin is thick and wrinkled, and is sparsely covered in short, sensory hairs. These hairs, or vibrissae, are highly sensitive and help the manatees navigate and detect changes in water currents and temperature, compensating for their relatively poor eyesight.

Manatees are herbivorous animals, eating more than 60 species of freshwater and saltwater plants, with seagrasses making up the majority of their diet. Because their diet is low in nutrients, they must consume vast quantities of food each day – up to 10-15% of their body weight. To feed, they use their highly flexible, prehensile lips to grab and pull plants into their mouths. Their teeth are unique: they have only molars, which are constantly replaced throughout their lives as they wear down from chewing sandy, abrasive vegetation. This process of continuous tooth replacement is known as "marching molars".

As mammals, manatees must breathe air. They typically surface every three to five minutes to breathe, though they can hold their breath for up to twenty minutes when resting. They breathe through nostrils located on the top of their snout, which are sealed by muscular flaps when they are underwater. To control their position in the water column, they use a highly developed buoyancy control system. Their lungs are long and flat, running along their back, and their bones are unusually dense and heavy, acting as natural weights to help them stay submerged while feeding on the bottom.

Despite their large size, manatees have very little body fat. This makes them highly sensitive to cold water. They cannot survive for long in water temperatures below 20 degrees Celsius. In winter, West Indian manatees migrate to warmer waters, often congregating in natural warm springs or in the warm-water discharge canals of coastal power plants. These warm-water refuges are critical to their survival, but they also expose the manatees to threats, such as collisions with boats and exposure to human activities.

Historically, manatees were hunted for their meat, hide, and oil. Today, they are protected by law in many countries, but they still face significant human-related threats. The most common cause of manatee injury and death is collisions with fast-moving watercraft. Their slow speed and tendency to feed in shallow waters make them particularly vulnerable to boat propellers. Other threats include habitat destruction, pollution, entanglement in fishing gear, and ingestion of marine debris. Conservation efforts focus on establishing "no-wake" speed zones for boats, protecting warm-water habitats, and rescuing and rehabilitating injured manatees.

Procrastination
Psychological analysis of task delays as a result of poor emotion regulation.

AWe’ve all been there: you have an important deadline looming, but instead of working, you find yourself organizing your bookshelf, scrolling through social media, or watching video clips online. This is procrastination – the voluntary delay of an intended task despite knowing that you will be worse off for the delay. For decades, procrastination was viewed primarily as a problem of time management or lack of willpower. However, modern psychological research has revealed a much more complex picture. Procrastination is now understood not as a sign of laziness, but as a mechanism for coping with negative emotions.

BWhen we face a task that makes us feel anxious, bored, insecure, or frustrated, our brain’s emotional center, the amygdala, perceives it as a threat. The amygdala triggers a stress response, urging us to escape the discomfort. Procrastinating provides immediate, temporary relief from these negative feelings. By choosing a pleasant activity over a demanding task, we successfully regulate our mood in the short term. The problem, of course, is that the task doesn’t go away, and the delay inevitably leads to greater stress, anxiety, and feelings of guilt in the long term, creating a vicious cycle.

CWhy do some people procrastinate more than others? Research suggests that certain personality traits are closely linked to procrastination. Perfectionists, for example, are highly vulnerable. Because they set exceptionally high standards for themselves, they are often paralyzed by the fear of failure, leading them to delay starting a task. People with low self-esteem or poor self-regulation skills also procrastinate more frequently. In contrast, highly conscientious individuals – those who are organized, self-disciplined, and goal-oriented – are much less likely to delay their work.

DThe consequences of chronic procrastination can be severe. In academic and professional settings, it leads to poorer performance, missed opportunities, and lower income. Chronic procrastinators are also more likely to suffer from stress-related health problems, such as headaches, digestive issues, and cardiovascular disease. Furthermore, the constant burden of unfinished tasks can damage relationships and lead to feelings of helplessness and depression. Procrastination is not just a harmless habit; it is a serious issue that can significantly reduce our well-being.

EFortunately, psychologists have developed effective strategies to help people overcome procrastination. One key approach is to change how we view the task. By breaking a large, overwhelming project into smaller, manageable steps, we can reduce the associated anxiety and make it easier to get started. Another strategy is to cultivate self-compassion. Studies show that people who forgive themselves for procrastinating in the past are less likely to procrastinate on future tasks. Being kind to yourself reduces the negative emotions that trigger procrastination in the first place.

FCreating a supportive environment is also crucial. This involves minimizing distractions, such as turning off notifications on your phone or blocking distracting websites while working. Techniques such as the Pomodoro Technique – working for 25 minutes followed by a five-minute break – can help to build focus and momentum. Ultimately, overcoming procrastination requires a shift in focus: instead of trying to manage our time, we must learn to manage our emotions.

Invasion of the Robot Umpires
The implementation of the Automated Ball-Strike System (ABS) and the debate on human judgment.

For more than a century, professional baseball has relied on human umpires to make split-second decisions about whether a pitch is a "ball" or a "strike". But that is changing. In recent years, Major League Baseball (MLB) has been testing the Automated Ball-Strike System (ABS), popularly known as "robot umpires". The system uses sophisticated camera-tracking technology to determine the exact path of the ball as it crosses the plate, transmitting the decision to the human umpire via an earpiece. The introduction of this technology has sparked a passionate debate about the role of human judgment in sports.

The core argument for ABS is accuracy and consistency. Human umpires, no matter how highly trained, are subject to errors, fatigue, and cognitive biases. Studies have shown that umpires miss a significant percentage of close calls, particularly at the edges of the strike zone. Furthermore, different umpires have different strike zones, leading to frustration for players and fans. The ABS system, by contrast, uses a mathematically defined, three-dimensional strike zone that is identical for every pitch. Proponents argue that this ensures fairness and eliminates the human errors that can alter the outcome of a game.

However, the transition to automated calling has not been entirely smooth. Players and umpires have raised concerns about the rigidity of the system. Umpires have pointed out that their role is not just to call balls and strikes, but to manage the game and defuse tensions. If their calling authority is stripped away, their overall control of the game could be diminished. Players, too, have struggled to adapt. The robotic strike zone is often wider or higher than the one humans typically call, leading to unexpected strikes. Some batters have complained that the system calls strikes on pitches that are technically in the zone but practically unhittable, changing the natural flow of the game.

There are also concerns about the impact of automation on the traditional character of baseball. Critics argue that human error is an inherent part of the sport, adding drama, debate, and a human element that fans connect with. They fear that a perfectly called game will feel sterile and mechanical, stripping the sport of its soul. Moreover, the skills of "pitch framing" – where a catcher subtly moves their mitt after catching a pitch to make it look like a strike – would become obsolete under ABS, devaluing a highly specialized human skill.

Despite these criticisms, MLB continues to refine and expand its use of ABS in the minor leagues, with the goal of eventually introducing it to Major League games. Several hybrid options are also being considered. One popular approach is the "challenge system", where human umpires continue to call pitches, but teams are allowed a limited number of video challenges per game. This allows for the correction of obvious mistakes while preserving the traditional role of the human umpire. As technology continues to advance, baseball is forced to confront a fundamental question: do we value absolute accuracy over the human elements of the game?
