Passage 1 – Georgia O’Keeffe
Georgia O’Keeffe
Seven-decade artistic career of modernist American painter Georgia O\'Keeffe.

Georgia O\'Keeffe (1887–1986) was a pioneering American modernist artist, renowned for her paintings of enlarged flowers, New York skyscrapers, and New Mexico landscapes. Over a career spanning seven decades, she created a unique body of work that challenged traditional ways of seeing and made her one of the most influential figures in 20th-century art. Her artistic journey took her from the plains of Texas to the towers of Manhattan, and ultimately to the high deserts of the American Southwest.

O\'Keeffe decided to become an artist at a young age. She studied at the School of the Art Institute of Chicago and the Art Students League of New York. However, she grew frustrated with the academic training, which focused on copying nature. In 1915, while working as an art teacher in Texas, she began experimenting with pure abstraction. She created a series of highly original charcoal drawings, using simple lines and curves to express her feelings. These drawings caught the attention of the famous photographer and art dealer Alfred Stieglitz, who exhibited them at his New York gallery in 1916. Stieglitz became O\'Keeffe’s champion, and later, her husband.

In the 1920s, O\'Keeffe lived in New York and painted dramatic, close-up views of skyscrapers. These paintings captured the energy and modernity of the rising city. But she also began painting the subjects that would define her career: flowers. By magnifying a single blossom – such as an iris, calla lily, or petunia – she forced viewers to look closely at its shapes and colors. O\'Keeffe denied that her flower paintings had any hidden symbolic meanings, insisting that they were simply about the beauty of form and color.

In 1929, O\'Keeffe traveled to New Mexico for the summer. The landscape, with its vast skies, rugged mountains, and brilliant light, immediately captivated her. From then on, she spent almost every summer in New Mexico, painting the dry hills, the adobe architecture, and the sun-bleached bones she collected in the desert. For O\'Keeffe, these bones were not symbols of death; rather, they were beautiful shapes that represented the enduring spirit of the desert. She painted them against the deep blue sky, sometimes floating in the air.

After Stieglitz’s death in 1946, O\'Keeffe moved permanently to New Mexico, living in her home in Abiquiú. She continued to paint, drawing inspiration from the cliffs and cottonwood trees. In her later years, her paintings became simpler and more abstract. She was inspired by views from airplane windows, painting vast series of clouds and winding rivers that stretched across the canvas. Even in her nineties, when failing eyesight made it difficult to paint, she continued to create art, working with clay and pencil with the help of assistants.

Today, Georgia O\'Keeffe is celebrated as a key figure in American modernism. Her houses in New Mexico have been preserved as museums, and her artwork is held in major collections worldwide. Her ability to find beauty in the simplest forms – a leaf, a bone, a shell – and to translate that beauty into powerful, abstract images remains her lasting legacy to the world.

Adapting to the effects of climate change
Global adaptive engineering and sustainability initiatives.

AAs global temperatures continue to rise, the impacts of climate change are becoming increasingly visible. Rising sea levels, more frequent and severe storms, and prolonged droughts are threatening communities around the world. In response, cities and countries are developing innovative engineering and ecological solutions to adapt to these changes. From high-tech barriers to traditional agricultural adaptations, these initiatives aim to build resilience and protect lives and livelihoods.

BOne major focus of adaptation is flood management. In Miami, Florida, sea-level rise is causing frequent "sunny day" flooding in low-lying neighborhoods. The city is responding by elevating roads, installing massive stormwater pumps, and requiring new buildings to be constructed on higher foundations. These measures are expensive, but necessary to prevent the city from becoming uninhabitable in the coming decades. Elsewhere, in the Mekong Delta of Vietnam, farmers are adapting to rising sea levels and saltwater intrusion by building buoyant houses that float during floods, allowing them to remain in place.

CIn rural areas, adaptation often involves restoring natural ecosystems. In the Solo River basin of East Java, Indonesia, farmers are working with conservationists to build bamboo dams. These simple, low-cost structures slow down water flow, reduce soil erosion, and help to trap sediment, which restores the fertility of the surrounding agricultural land. The bamboo dams also help to recharge groundwater supplies, providing a reliable source of water during the dry season. This community-led initiative shows that effective adaptation does not always require expensive, high-tech solutions.

DIn coastal Bangladesh, climate change is causing increased salinity in agricultural soils, making it impossible to grow traditional crops. In response, farmers are turning to salt-tolerant crops, such as specific varieties of rice and vegetables. This adaptation allows them to maintain food security and earn an income despite the changing environmental conditions. In addition, many farmers are constructing raised garden beds, known as "floating gardens", made of water hyacinth plants, which rise and fall with the water levels, protecting crops from floods.

EIn urban centers, adaptation can also help to mitigate the "urban heat island" effect. In Los Angeles, California, the city is implementing a plan to cool neighborhoods by painting pavements with a reflective gray coating. This coating reduces the surface temperature of the streets by several degrees, lowering the ambient temperature of the surrounding area. The city is also expanding its urban forest by planting thousands of trees, which provide shade and cool the air through evapotranspiration. These initiatives improve the comfort and health of residents during extreme heat events.

FGiven the scale of the climate crisis, adaptation must be integrated into all levels of planning and development. Successful initiatives require collaboration between scientists, engineers, local communities, and policymakers. By combining modern technology with traditional knowledge and ecological restoration, we can build a more resilient future. The challenge is immense, but the cost of inaction is far greater.

A new role for livestock guard dogs

How traditional livestock guard dogs are helping to protect both shepherds’ herds and predator species.

AFor centuries, shepherds in Europe and Asia used large, fierce dogs to protect their sheep and goats from wolves, bears, and other predators. These guard dogs, such as the Anatolian Shepherd and Maremma, were not trained to herd the livestock, but to live among them and defend them from threat. However, as large predators were eradicated from much of their historical ranges, the use of guard dogs declined. Today, as conservation efforts lead to the recovery of predator populations, these ancient dog breeds are finding a new role – not just in protecting livestock, but in conserving the very predators they were bred to fight.

BIn many parts of the world, the recovery of large predators is met with anger and resistance from farmers and ranchers. When wolves, cheetahs, or jaguars attack livestock, farmers often respond by shooting, poisoning, or trapping the predators. This human-predator conflict is a major threat to the survival of many endangered carnivores. By providing farmers with an effective, non-lethal way to protect their herds, guard dogs can significantly reduce livestock losses, thereby reducing the farmers’ hostility toward predators and preventing retaliatory killings.

CIn Namibia, the Cheetah Conservation Fund (CCF) has been running a highly successful Livestock Guarding Dog program since 1994. The CCF breeds Anatolian Shepherds and Kangals – breeds known for their size, courage, and ability to withstand the hot, dry climate – and places them with local farmers. The dogs are raised from puppies with the goats and sheep, bonding closely with the herd. When a predator, such as a cheetah or leopard, approaches, the dog barks loudly and places itself between the threat and the herd. Most predators are deterred by the dog’s size and loud bark and choose to hunt wild prey instead. The program has reduced livestock losses by over 80% for participating farmers, significantly improving the survival prospects of Namibia’s cheetahs.

DThe benefits of guard dogs extend beyond the protection of livestock. Studies have shown that the presence of these large dogs can alter the behavior of smaller, mesopredators, such as jackals and foxes. In the absence of large predators, these smaller carnivores often experience a population boom, leading to increased predation on wild birds, small mammals, and livestock. Guard dogs help to regulate these smaller predators, restoring a more balanced ecological structure. Furthermore, the use of dogs can prevent the spread of diseases from wild carnivores to domestic herds, as the dogs keep wild animals at a safe distance.

EHowever, implementing a guard dog program is not without challenges. These dogs are large, powerful animals that require proper care, feeding, and medical attention. Shepherds must be trained in how to manage the dogs, and neighbors must be aware of their presence. Furthermore, not all dogs are suited for the work; some may show too much aggression toward humans or livestock, while others may lack the necessary protective instincts. Successful programs require ongoing support from conservation organizations to ensure the health and effectiveness of the dogs.

FDespite these challenges, the use of livestock guard dogs is expanding worldwide. Programs are now active in the United States, Australia, and several European countries, helping to ease the reintroduction of wolves and bears. By bridging the gap between agriculture and conservation, these dogs are showing that it is possible for humans and wild predators to coexist. The ancient partnership between shepherds and dogs is proving to be a modern tool for ecological harmony.
